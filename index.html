Summary

Order Psittaciformes

Identification

Appearance

The kākāpō is a large flightless parrot in New Zealand. It is also known as the owl parrot. This beautiful bird is green all over with yellow and black spots speckled through its feathers. It has a grey beak, legs, and feet, with their underside being a lighter yellow colour. They don’t have any living relatives, but the closest relatives to kākāpōs are keas and kākās. They weigh up to 4 kg with a height of about 60 cm. They can add on more weight during the breeding season. Both their wingspan and their tail are relatively small for their size. This adaptation is due to their lack of natural predators which has allowed them to gain weight. They now have much stronger legs and beaks for foraging rather than large wings and tails for flying.

Habitat

Once walking across all of New Zealand, kākāpōs now live exclusively in forested islands of New Zealand. They used to have a much broader range but predation has made their distribution greatly reduce. They make nests in places that are hidden: underground, in hollow tree trunks, or areas with thick vegetation. They have their own territory and they stay within this home range for most of their life.

Behaviour

In their day to day life, when trying to conceal themselves, kākāpōs use their feathers as camouflage on the forest floors. They are nocturnal, so they sleep during the day and forage for food in the safety of darkness. The will walk on the ground and even climb up trees to reach their food. Because of their short wings, they cannot fly, but they can manage to glide to the next tree. Their call can be loud and high pitched, or low and booming for their recognizable mating displays.

Diet

Kākāpōs have a completely plant-based diet. As a bird that cannot fly, they climb their way up trees and search the ground for food. Their main source of food is the rimu fruit from New Zealand’s rimu trees. As they forage the ground, the birds chew on these fruits and extract the nutrients, leaving behind a wad of the chewed-up fruit. Other sources of their nutrients come from fruits, seeds, nuts, berries, leaves, bark, and tubers. They don’t have any natural predators, but many have been introduced into the land that endangers their population.

Mating

Newborn chicks look like white fluffs. They are artricial, meaning they are not fully developed when they hatch. They stay with the mother for approximately 70 days to be fed and taken care of. At this point they start fledging and getting used to the bird life. After about 9 months, they are independent. They start breeding at the age of six years. Only the females take care of the young. Kākāpōs are lek breeders. This system of breeding is when males compete with each other in front of females to attract a mate. Kākāpō males pick an area to display their mating show and clear out this land to make their "track and bowl system". They then let out their booming mating calls and walk around their stage to get chosen by a spectating female. They breed during the spring season, typically when their favourite rimu fruit is abundant. The female lays a clutch of 1-4 eggs, all white at about 50 mm long and 38 mm wide. The eggs are incubated for a month and then they start to hatch. They have one of the longest life spans of birds, living an average of 80 years. Some have been thought to live close to 100 years.

Endangerment

History

During the 1300s, Polynesians known as Maori came to New Zealand and settled there. The kākāpōs lived all over New Zealand then, so they were hunted by the Maori. Their meat became food and their feathers were made into cloaks. They also brought animals with them, such as rats and dogs. The dogs became predators to the bird and the rats attacked its eggs and young chicks when the mother left to find food. Later in the 1800s, European settlers had begun to colonize New Zealand. This shrunk down the kākāpōs’ natural habitat further with deforestation. They also brought more animals with them; cats, stoats, and even more rats. All these troubles for the poor kākāpō have led their population to shrink down to only about 150 of them left in the wild.

Effects

The introduced mammals have a strong sense of smell. This exposes the kākāpōs as they can’t protect themselves with camouflage anymore like they would have done to hide from other big birds of prey. Their population got as low as only 50 living during the mid-1900s. Low population has forced the kākāpōs to inbreed. This has decreased the fertility rate and genetic diversity of their eggs which puts their chance of survival into further risk. Through conservation efforts, they have moved from the brink of extinction to critical endangerment. Roughly only 200 kākāpōs are alive now.

Saving the Birds

The main goal in conserving this species was to get them away from their predators. This was attempted several times. In the late 1800s, the kākāpōs were relocated to Resolution island in Fiordland. This attempt was unsuccessful because soon enough stoats had found their way to the island and the birds were in danger again. Mid-1900s, people in New Zealand had started a mission to find and revive what was remaining of the kākāpō population. Twenty to thirty kākāpōs were found, but none of them were female. Further along in the 1900s, a large group of kākāpōs were found on Stewart Island, away from all their threatening predators, and with females. This was great hope for their population. They were relocated to safe islands such as Little Barrier island. Stoats and rats are still a threat to the birds as they prey on the eggs. The predators were eradicated in some islands but the birds must still be protected if they reappear. Majority, if not all of the kākāpōs, are known now and they are being monitored with radio transmitters to make sure they continue to survive.
