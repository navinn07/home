Identification

Appearance

The kākāpōs is a large flightless parrot in New Zeleand. It is also known as the owl parrot, part of the Psittaciformes order that encompases all parrots. This beautiful bird is green all over with yellow and black spots speckled through its feathers. They don’t have any living relatives, but the closest relative to kākāpōs are keas and kākās.

Diet

They have a completely plant-based diet. As a bird that cannot fly, they climb their way up trees and search the ground for food like berries and leaves. They don’t have any natural predators, but many have been introduced into the land that endangers their population.

Life Cycle

They have one of the longest lifespans of birds, living an average of 100 years. They are nocturnal.

Endagerment

History and Reasons

There are a few reasons, mainly habitat loss and predation. When European settlers decided to colonize New Zeleand, they brought animals with them, such as rats and felines. These animals became either predators to the bird or attacked its eggs and young chicks when the mother left to find food. And of course, as more people settled on the land and grew their territories, the birds’ habitats began to disappear. All these troubles for the poor kākāpōs have led their population to shrink down to only about 150 of them left in the wild.

Effects

Low population has forced the kākāpōs to inbreed. This has decreased the fertility rate of their eggs which puts their chance of survival into more risk.
